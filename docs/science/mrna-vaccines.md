# mRNA Vaccines

## What Is mRNA?

If DNA is the master instruction book for the body, **mRNA** (messenger RNA) is like a photocopy of a single page. It carries instructions from DNA to the cell's protein-making machinery: "make this specific protein."

mRNA is temporary — the cell reads it, makes the protein, and then breaks down the mRNA. It does **not** change your pet's DNA.

## How mRNA Vaccines Work

You may be familiar with mRNA from the COVID-19 vaccines. The concept for cancer is similar but with a different goal:

### COVID mRNA Vaccine (preventive)
- Contains mRNA encoding the spike protein from the coronavirus
- Cells make the spike protein briefly
- The immune system learns to recognize it
- If the real virus shows up later, the immune system is ready

### Cancer mRNA Vaccine (therapeutic)
- Contains mRNA encoding **neoantigens** from your pet's specific tumor
- Cells make those neoantigen peptides briefly
- The immune system learns to recognize them as abnormal
- The immune system then hunts for cells displaying those neoantigens — the cancer cells

## Why mRNA for Cancer Vaccines?

mRNA technology has several advantages for personalized cancer vaccines:

1. **Fast to design** — Once you know the neoantigen sequences, designing the mRNA is a computational problem
2. **Fast to manufacture** — mRNA can be synthesized in days, not months
3. **Customizable** — Each vaccine is unique to the patient's tumor
4. **Multi-target** — A single mRNA strand can encode multiple neoantigens, reducing the chance the cancer escapes by losing one target
5. **Safe** — mRNA is naturally broken down by the body and does not alter DNA

## Important Limitations

- mRNA vaccine synthesis requires specialized laboratory equipment
- The vaccine must be properly formulated (usually with lipid nanoparticles) for delivery
- Administration should only be done under veterinary supervision
- Personalized cancer vaccines are experimental — they show promise but are not guaranteed to work

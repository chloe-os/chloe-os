# What Are Neoantigens?

## The Immune System's "Most Wanted" List

Your pet's immune system constantly patrols the body, looking for anything that doesn't belong — viruses, bacteria, and abnormal cells. It recognizes threats by examining small protein fragments (called **peptides**) displayed on the surface of cells.

Normal cells display normal protein fragments. But cancer cells, because of their mutations, can display **abnormal** protein fragments. These abnormal fragments are called **neoantigens** — new antigens that exist only because of the cancer's unique mutations.

## Why Neoantigens Matter

Neoantigens are the foundation of personalized cancer vaccines because they are:

1. **Unique to the tumor** — They don't exist in healthy tissue, so targeting them doesn't harm normal cells
2. **Truly foreign** — Because they come from mutated proteins, the immune system can learn to recognize them as dangerous
3. **Specific to your pet's cancer** — Every tumor has its own set of mutations, so every tumor has its own neoantigens

## How Chloe OS Finds Neoantigens

Not every mutation creates a good neoantigen. Chloe OS evaluates mutations based on several criteria:

1. **Does the mutation change a protein?** Some mutations are "silent" — they don't change the protein and can't create neoantigens
2. **Can the mutant peptide bind to MHC molecules?** MHC molecules are what cells use to display peptides on their surface. If a peptide can't bind to MHC, the immune system won't see it (see [MHC Binding](mhc-binding.md))
3. **Does the mutant peptide bind better than the normal version?** Ideally, the mutant peptide should be more visible to the immune system than the normal one
4. **How common is the mutation in the tumor?** Mutations present in more cancer cells are better targets

## From Neoantigen to Vaccine

Once promising neoantigens are identified, they can be encoded in an mRNA vaccine. See [mRNA Vaccines](mrna-vaccines.md) for how this works.

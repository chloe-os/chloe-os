# Canine Immunology

## Dogs Get Cancer Too

Cancer is the leading cause of death in dogs over 10 years old. Some breeds are particularly susceptible:

- **Golden Retrievers** — High rates of lymphoma and hemangiosarcoma
- **Boxers** — Prone to mast cell tumors and brain tumors
- **Bernese Mountain Dogs** — High incidence of histiocytic sarcoma
- **Rottweilers** — Predisposed to osteosarcoma
- **Staffordshire Bull Terriers** — Common mast cell tumors

## The Canine Immune System

Dogs have an immune system very similar to humans, including:

- **T cells** — Kill infected or abnormal cells
- **B cells** — Produce antibodies
- **Natural killer cells** — Target cells that don't display MHC properly
- **Dendritic cells** — Present antigens to T cells to initiate immune responses

This similarity is what makes personalized cancer vaccines a plausible approach for dogs.

## DLA — The Dog's MHC System

**DLA (Dog Leukocyte Antigen)** is the canine equivalent of the human HLA system. The most important gene for cancer vaccines is **DLA-88**, the most polymorphic MHC class I gene in dogs.

DLA-88 comes in many variants (alleles), and different breeds tend to carry different alleles. This matters because different DLA alleles bind different peptides — so the right neoantigen target depends on your dog's specific DLA type.

## Why Dogs Are Important for Cancer Research

Research on canine cancer benefits both dogs and humans:

1. **Dogs develop cancer naturally** — Unlike lab mice, dogs get cancer spontaneously, making their tumors more similar to human cancers
2. **Shared environments** — Dogs live in the same environments as humans and face similar carcinogenic exposures
3. **Similar genetics** — Many cancer genes are shared between dogs and humans
4. **Faster timelines** — Clinical outcomes in dogs can be observed faster than in human trials

Advances in personalized veterinary oncology have the potential to accelerate human cancer research as well.

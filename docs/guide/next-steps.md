# Next Steps

You have your Chloe OS report. Here's what to do with it.

## 1. Share with Your Veterinarian

**This is the most important step.** Your veterinarian or veterinary oncologist needs to review the results and help you understand what they mean for your specific pet.

Bring the report (printed or on a screen) and discuss:

- Whether the findings are relevant to your pet's cancer type
- How this fits with your pet's current treatment plan
- Whether pursuing a personalized vaccine makes sense

## 2. Understand the Limitations

Before getting excited about the results, remember:

- These are **predictions**, not guarantees
- Not every predicted neoantigen will actually trigger an immune response
- Personalized cancer vaccines are **experimental** — there are success stories, but also many cases of no response
- The binding predictions for canine MHC (DLA) are less validated than for human MHC

## 3. Find a Research Partner

If your vet thinks the results are promising and you want to explore further, you'll need a laboratory partner to:

- Review the neoantigen candidates
- Design and synthesize an mRNA vaccine construct
- Administer the vaccine under veterinary supervision

Good places to start:

- **University veterinary schools** — Many have oncology research programs
- **mRNA research labs** — Labs working on RNA vaccine technology
- **Veterinary clinical trials** — Check if there are any relevant trials your pet could join

## 4. Continue Standard Treatment

!!! warning "Critical"
    **Do not stop or delay proven treatments** (surgery, chemotherapy, radiation) in favor of an experimental approach. Personalized vaccines should be considered as a potential *complement* to standard treatment, not a replacement.

## 5. Share Your Experience (Optional)

If you're comfortable, you can contribute anonymized data from your analysis to the Chloe OS community dataset:

```bash
chloe community submit --report your_report.html
```

This helps build a collective knowledge base of canine cancer neoantigens that benefits future pet owners and researchers.
